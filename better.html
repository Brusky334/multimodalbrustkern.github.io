Section 01

Why Quantum Computers
Are So Much Better

The difference between a classical bit and a qubit isn't just faster — it's a fundamentally different model of computation.

Superposition: Being in two places at once

A classical bit is a switch — it's either off (0) or on (1). Every computation your laptop does is ultimately a massive cascade of these binary decisions. The problem is that for certain problems — like searching a vast database, or factoring a very large number — you have to check possibilities one at a time.

A qubit exploits quantum superposition: until measured, it exists in a combination of both 0 and 1 simultaneously. With 4 qubits, you can represent all 16 possible 4-bit values at the same time. With 300 qubits, you can hold more states simultaneously than there are atoms in the observable universe.

Entanglement: Instant correlation across particles

When two qubits are "entangled," the state of one instantly determines the state of the other — no matter the physical distance between them. This allows quantum computers to perform coordinated operations across many qubits simultaneously, creating a kind of parallelism that has no classical equivalent.

Entanglement is what allows quantum algorithms like Shor's (for factoring) and Grover's (for search) to achieve their dramatic speedups. The qubits don't just work in parallel — they interfere with each other in mathematically precise ways that amplify the probability of the right answer and cancel out wrong ones.

Interference: Canceling out wrong answers

Quantum algorithms are designed so that paths leading to incorrect answers destructively interfere — they cancel each other out, like opposite waves colliding. Paths leading to the correct answer constructively interfere, building up probability until measurement almost certainly returns the right result.

This is the mechanism that makes quantum computers fast for the right kind of problem. It's not random luck — it's engineered interference. The algorithm is a precisely choreographed dance of waves.
