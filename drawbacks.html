Section 02

Current Drawbacks

Quantum computers are extraordinarily fragile. Understanding why requires understanding what qubits are made of — and why each approach carries its own engineering nightmare.

The three dominant qubit architectures

There is no single agreed-upon way to build a qubit. Each physical implementation has unique strengths and unique challenges that have made building reliable quantum computers so difficult.

Type 01

Superconducting Qubits

Used by Google and IBM, these are circuits cooled to near absolute zero (–273°C) that exhibit quantum behavior. They are fast and manufacturable using adapted semiconductor techniques, but require enormous refrigeration infrastructure and lose coherence in microseconds.


Key challenge: decoherence time (~100 μs)

Type 02

Trapped Ion Qubits

Individual ions are suspended in electromagnetic fields and manipulated with lasers. They offer exceptional coherence times (seconds to minutes) and near-perfect gate fidelity, but operating speeds are much slower and scaling to thousands of qubits has proven extremely difficult.

Key challenge: gate speed and scaling

Type 03

Photonic Qubits

These use individual photons (particles of light) as qubits. Photons naturally resist decoherence and can travel through optical fiber, making them promising for quantum networking. However, making photons interact with each other is exceptionally hard, which makes two-qubit gate operations unreliable.

Key challenge: two-qubit gate fidelity

The noise problem

Every qubit architecture shares one fundamental enemy: noise. Any interaction with the outside environment — a stray photon, a tiny vibration, even cosmic rays — can "collapse" a qubit's superposition and corrupt the computation. This phenomenon is called decoherence.

Today's machines are called NISQ devices: Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum computers. They have tens to thousands of qubits, but every qubit is error-prone. Running long calculations before errors accumulate is the central engineering challenge of this decade.

Quantum error correction requires massive overhead

The solution to noise is quantum error correction (QEC): using many physical qubits to encode a single, reliable "logical" qubit. Current estimates suggest you may need 1,000 to 10,000 physical qubits to produce one error-corrected logical qubit suitable for serious computation.

This means a quantum computer capable of breaking modern encryption would need millions of physical, low-error qubits. We currently have thousands. The gap is large — but the trajectory of progress suggests it is closable within the next two to three decades.

Temperature and infrastructure

Superconducting quantum computers, the most advanced today, must operate at temperatures colder than deep space — around 15 millikelvins, roughly 250 times colder than the cosmic microwave background. This requires specialized dilution refrigerators the size of a wardrobe that cost hundreds of thousands of dollars.

This is not a fundamental obstacle — engineering solutions are being developed to miniaturize and scale the cooling systems. But it does mean that near-term quantum computers will be cloud-accessed data center machines, not desktop devices or consumer hardware.
