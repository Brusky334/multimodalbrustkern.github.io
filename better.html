Superposition: Being in two places at once

A classical bit is like a light switch, it's either off (0) or on (1). Every computation your laptop or phone does is ultimately a massive series of these binary decisions. The issue is that for certain problems — like searching a vast database, or factoring an extremely large number — you have to check possibilities one at a time.

Qubits can use what is called quantum superposition: until called upon, it exists in a combination of both 0 and 1 at the same time. With 4 qubits, you can represent all 16 possible 4-bit values at the same time. With 300 qubits, you can hold more states simultaneously than there are atoms in the observable universe.

Entanglement: Instant correlation across particles

When two qubits are "entangled," the state of one instantly determines the state of the other. The physical distance between them does not matter. This allows quantum computers to perform synced operations across many qubits at the same time, creating a kind of similarity that no classical computer has.

Entanglement is what allows quantum algorithms like Shor's (for factoring) and Grover's (for search) to achieve their accelerate results. The qubits don't just work side by side, they mess with each other in precise ways that aincrease the probability of the right answer and cancel out the wrong ones.

Interference: Canceling out wrong answers

Quantum algorithms are designed so that paths leading to incorrect answers automatically end, they cancel each other out, like opposite waves colliding. Paths leading to the correct answer constructively interfere, building up probability until called upon, making the algorithm return the correct result practically every time.

This is the process that makes quantum computers quicker for the right kind of problems. It's not random luck, it's calculated interference. The algorithm is a precisely planned pattern of waves.
